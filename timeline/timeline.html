1873

Born on April 1, 1873

April 1 (March 20, Old Style), 1873, in Semyonovo, Russia

1882

Starts piano lessons with his mother, Anna, in 1882

At the age of just nine, Sergei Rachmaninoff embarked on his musical journey by beginning piano lessons with his mother, Anna. This early exposure to music would lay the foundation for his remarkable talent and future achievements as a composer and pianist. Under his mother's guidance, Rachmaninoff developed his piano skills and displayed a natural aptitude for music, setting the stage for a brilliant career in the world of classical music.

1885

Moves to Saint Petersburg in 1885

His family moves to Saint Petersburg, where Rachmaninoff studies at the Saint Petersburg Conservatory. Under the guidance of renowned composers and music instructors, he honed his skills as a pianist and composer. His innate talent, coupled with rigorous training, laid the foundation for his future success as a composer and performer.

1889

Graduates from the Conservatory with honors in 1889

In 1889, Sergei Rachmaninoff achieved a significant milestone in his musical education when he graduated from the Moscow Conservatory with exceptional honors. His graduation marked not only the culmination of years of rigorous training but also the recognition of his extraordinary talent as a pianist and composer.

During his time at the conservatory, Rachmaninoff studied under some of the most prominent musicians and composers of his era, further honing his skills and refining his unique musical style. His graduation with honors was a testament to his dedication and exceptional abilities, foreshadowing a brilliant career in the world of classical music.

1891

Rachmaninoff composed his first piece, "Prelude in C-sharp minor" in 1891

In the year 1891, at the age of 18, Sergei Rachmaninoff achieved a significant milestone in his musical career by composing his very first piece, the iconic "Prelude in C-sharp minor." This composition marked the beginning of Rachmaninoff's prolific journey as a composer. The Prelude, with its hauntingly beautiful melodies and rich harmonies, quickly gained recognition for its emotional depth and technical brilliance. It foreshadowed the exceptional composer he would become, known for his romantic and expressive compositions that continue to captivate audiences around the world.

1892

Premieres his First Piano Concerto in F-sharp minor in 1892

In 1892, at the age of just 19, Sergei Rachmaninoff achieved a significant milestone in his budding career as a composer and pianist. He premiered his First Piano Concerto in F-sharp minor, a composition that would later become one of his most celebrated works. This concerto showcased Rachmaninoff's exceptional piano skills and his unique ability to blend Romantic melodies with virtuosic passages.

The premiere of this concerto marked the beginning of Rachmaninoff's reputation as a composer of great promise. Although the initial performance faced its share of challenges, including a poorly prepared conductor and a hasty rehearsal schedule, the concerto eventually gained recognition for its lush melodies and expressive depth.

Rachmaninoff's First Piano Concerto is now considered a staple of the classical piano repertoire, admired for its lyrical beauty and technical demands. It serves as a testament to the young composer's talent and the enduring legacy he would leave on the world of classical music.

1892

Settles in Moscow in 1892

In 1892, Sergei Rachmaninoff made a significant move in his life by settling in Moscow, the capital of Russia. This marked a pivotal moment in his career as a composer and pianist. Moscow was a thriving center of arts and culture, and it provided Rachmaninoff with an environment that was rich in musical opportunities and inspiration.

During his time in Moscow, Rachmaninoff furthered his musical studies and became deeply involved in the city's vibrant music scene. His presence in the cultural hub of Moscow allowed him to collaborate with renowned musicians and composers, contributing to his growth as an artist.

1894

Completes his Symphony No. 1 in 1894, which receives a disastrous premiere

In 1894, Sergei Rachmaninoff completed his Symphony No. 1, a significant milestone in his early compositional career. However, the premiere of this symphony was marred by controversy and disappointment. When it was first performed, the audience and critics reacted with harsh criticism, and the reception was nothing short of disastrous. This harsh reception deeply affected Rachmaninoff, plunging him into a period of self-doubt and depression. It was a challenging moment in his life, and the symphony's failure weighed heavily on him.

1897

Falls into a deep depression after the failure of his First Symphony in 1897

In 1897, Sergei Rachmaninoff faced a profound personal and professional crisis when his First Symphony received a disappointing and harsh reception at its premiere. The critical failure of this work left Rachmaninoff devastated and plunged him into a deep depression. The negative reviews weighed heavily on his self-confidence and artistic spirit.

During this challenging period in his life, Rachmaninoff struggled to find inspiration and questioned his abilities as a composer. His depression and self-doubt cast a shadow over his creative endeavors, making it difficult for him to compose new music.

Despite these difficulties, Rachmaninoff would later find renewed inspiration and resilience, ultimately composing some of his most iconic and beloved works that would secure his place as one of the greatest composers and pianists of the 20th century.

1901

Composes the famous Prelude in C-sharp minor in 1901

In the year 1901, Sergei Rachmaninoff composed what would become one of his most iconic and celebrated works, the Prelude in C-sharp minor. This hauntingly beautiful and emotionally charged composition is a testament to Rachmaninoff's genius as a composer and his ability to evoke deep emotions through music.

The Prelude in C-sharp minor quickly gained recognition for its melancholic and introspective melodies, and it remains a beloved piece in the classical piano repertoire to this day. Rachmaninoff's mastery of harmonies and rich textures is evident in this composition, which showcases his unique style and emotional depth.

1902

Completes his Second Piano Concerto in C minor in 1902

In 1902, Sergei Rachmaninoff achieved a significant milestone in his career by completing his renowned Second Piano Concerto in C minor. This masterpiece is celebrated for its captivating melodies, lush harmonies, and virtuosic piano passages, making it one of Rachmaninoff's most beloved compositions. The concerto reflects his remarkable ability as both a composer and a pianist, showcasing his profound understanding of the instrument's capabilities.

Rachmaninoff's Second Piano Concerto has since become a staple of the classical repertoire, cherished by audiences and musicians alike for its emotional depth and technical brilliance. Its enduring popularity continues to captivate listeners and stands as a testament to Rachmaninoff's enduring legacy in the world of classical music.

1904

Marries Natalia Satina in 1904

In 1904, Sergei Rachmaninoff entered a new chapter in his personal life by marrying Natalia Satina. This union marked an important milestone in his journey, as Natalia became a source of support and inspiration throughout his career. Their marriage provided Rachmaninoff with stability and happiness amid the challenges of the changing political and cultural landscape of the time.

1906

Leaves Russia due to the political and social turmoil, settling in Dresden, Germany, in 1906

In 1906, Sergei Rachmaninoff made a momentous decision to leave his homeland of Russia. This pivotal move was driven by the political and social turmoil that had engulfed Russia during the early 20th century. The tumultuous events of the time, including the Russian Revolution of 1905, had left a profound impact on Rachmaninoff's life and artistic pursuits.

Seeking stability and an environment conducive to creativity, Rachmaninoff chose to settle in Dresden, Germany. This relocation marked a significant chapter in his life, as it allowed him to continue his musical career and compose some of his most celebrated works in a more tranquil setting.

1909

Completes his Symphony No. 2 in E minor in 1909

In 1909, Sergei Rachmaninoff achieved a significant milestone in his composing career by completing his Symphony No. 2 in E minor. This symphony is considered one of Rachmaninoff's most celebrated works and a testament to his compositional prowess. The Symphony No. 2 is known for its lush melodies, emotional depth, and remarkable orchestration, reflecting Rachmaninoff's signature romantic style.

It was a critical and popular success, marking a triumphant comeback after the initial disappointment of his Symphony No. 1. The completion of Symphony No. 2 reaffirmed Rachmaninoff's position as a prominent figure in the world of classical music and further solidified his reputation as a composer of great distinction.

1910

Tours the United States as a pianist in 1910

In 1910, Sergei Rachmaninoff embarked on a landmark tour of the United States as a virtuoso pianist, marking a significant chapter in his musical career. This tour introduced American audiences to the extraordinary talents of the Russian composer and pianist. Rachmaninoff's performances across the country were met with critical acclaim and enthusiasm from audiences, solidifying his reputation as one of the foremost pianists of his time.

1915

Completes the Études-Tableaux, Op. 39 in 1915

In the year 1915, Sergei Rachmaninoff completed one of his notable piano compositions, the Études-Tableaux, Op. 39. This collection of musical pieces for the piano showcases Rachmaninoff's profound artistry and technical prowess. The Études-Tableaux, Op. 39, is renowned for its intricate musical textures, rich harmonies, and emotive depth.

These compositions are characterized by their evocative titles, each suggesting a mood or scene. Rachmaninoff's ability to convey deep emotions through his music is particularly evident in this opus. The Études-Tableaux, Op. 39, stands as a testament to his mastery of the piano and his skill in capturing the human experience through music.

1917

The Russian Revolution further disrupts his connection to his homeland in 1917

In 1917, the Russian Revolution marked a pivotal moment in Sergei Rachmaninoff's life and career. The political upheaval and societal changes brought about by the revolution had profound consequences for the composer and his relationship with his homeland, Russia.

As the revolution unfolded, Rachmaninoff, like many artists and intellectuals of his time, found himself in a precarious situation. The revolutionary forces and the ensuing turmoil threatened the stability and cultural fabric of Russia. Rachmaninoff's connection to the country of his birth was further disrupted as the revolution led to social and political unrest, making it increasingly difficult for him to maintain ties with Russia.

1918

Returns to Russia after the October Revolution in 1918

Following the turmoil of the October Revolution in Russia, Sergei Rachmaninoff made the poignant decision to return to his homeland in 1918. The revolution had brought about significant political and social upheaval, and Rachmaninoff's return during this tumultuous period was marked by a deep sense of attachment to his country and a desire to reconnect with his Russian roots.

Rachmaninoff's return was not without challenges. He witnessed the profound changes that had taken place in Russia since his departure and experienced the effects of the revolution firsthand. Despite the difficulties, he remained committed to his music and continued to compose during this period, leaving a lasting impact on the world of classical music.

1919

Composes his Symphonic Dances in 1919

In the year 1919, Sergei Rachmaninoff created one of his notable compositions, the "Symphonic Dances." This work marked a significant moment in Rachmaninoff's career as a composer. The Symphonic Dances, composed in the twilight of his creative output, exemplify his mature and reflective style.

The Symphonic Dances are a three-movement orchestral suite that showcases Rachmaninoff's gift for crafting memorable melodies and lush harmonies. This composition displays his ability to blend Russian and Western musical elements, resulting in a rich and evocative piece that has earned its place among his most celebrated works.

1921

Leaves Russia for the last time due to the political climate in 1921

In 1921, Sergei Rachmaninoff made the difficult and fateful decision to leave his homeland, Russia, for the final time. This departure was primarily driven by the tumultuous political climate that had engulfed Russia following the Russian Revolution of 1917 and the subsequent Russian Civil War. The political upheaval and societal changes had a profound impact on Rachmaninoff's life and artistic freedom.

With great reluctance and a heavy heart, Rachmaninoff left his beloved Russia, bidding farewell to his homeland and the cultural heritage that had shaped his music. He embarked on a new chapter in his life, eventually settling in various countries, including Switzerland and the United States, where he continued to compose and perform, leaving an indelible mark on the world of classical music.

1925

Settles in Switzerland in 1925

In 1925, Sergei Rachmaninoff made the decision to settle in Switzerland, marking a significant chapter in his life. Switzerland, with its serene landscapes and peaceful atmosphere, provided Rachmaninoff with a tranquil environment where he could focus on his music and composition. This period in Switzerland allowed him to continue his creative work, composing some of his renowned pieces, and further solidifying his status as a prominent composer and pianist in the world of classical music.

1930

Moves to the United States, eventually settling in New York City, in 1930

In 1930, Sergei Rachmaninoff made a life-changing decision to leave Europe and relocate to the United States. The move came as a result of several factors, including political turmoil in Europe and economic opportunities in America. Rachmaninoff, along with his family, embarked on a new chapter in their lives by crossing the Atlantic Ocean.

1931

Becomes a U.S. citizen in 1931

In 1931, Sergei Rachmaninoff took a significant step in his life by becoming a United States citizen. This decision marked a pivotal moment in his career and personal life. After emigrating from Russia due to the tumultuous political climate and the Russian Revolution, Rachmaninoff had spent several years in various European countries, including Switzerland and Germany. However, he eventually made the United States his permanent home.

1934

Composes his Rhapsody on a Theme of Paganini in 1934

In 1934, Sergei Rachmaninoff composed one of his most celebrated and enduring works, the "Rhapsody on a Theme of Paganini." This composition is a set of 24 variations for piano and orchestra, based on the famous and hauntingly beautiful theme originally composed by the legendary violinist and composer Niccolò Paganini.

Rachmaninoff's interpretation of Paganini's theme showcases his virtuosic piano skills and his ability to create mesmerizing melodies and intricate variations. The piece seamlessly blends classical and romantic elements, making it a captivating and beloved composition in the world of classical music.

The "Rhapsody on a Theme of Paganini" is known for its lyrical and expressive qualities, and it has been performed and recorded by countless pianists and orchestras worldwide. It stands as a testament to Rachmaninoff's mastery as both a composer and a pianist, leaving a lasting legacy in the realm of classical music.

1940

Completes his Symphony No. 3 in A minor in 1940

In 1940, Sergei Rachmaninoff achieved a remarkable milestone in his illustrious career as a composer with the completion of his Symphony No. 3 in A minor. This symphony, often regarded as one of his late masterpieces, showcases Rachmaninoff's continued dedication to the world of classical music despite the challenges he faced during his emigration from Russia and subsequent life in the United States.

Rachmaninoff's Symphony No. 3 is characterized by its lush melodies, rich orchestration, and emotional depth. It stands as a testament to his enduring creative spirit and his ability to blend elements of Russian romanticism with modern influences. The symphony received critical acclaim and further solidified Rachmaninoff's reputation as a leading composer of the 20th century.

While Rachmaninoff's career was marked by numerous compositions for piano and orchestra, his Symphony No. 3 holds a special place in the classical repertoire, exemplifying his continued passion for composing and his unwavering commitment to the world of music.

1943

Sergei Rachmaninoff passes away on March 28, 1943

On March 28, 1943, the world bid farewell to one of the greatest composers and pianists of the 20th century, Sergei Rachmaninoff. He passed away in Beverly Hills, California, marking the end of a remarkable life dedicated to music and art.
